Day 1 · What TBL actually means
Subject line
What "Team-Based Learning" actually means (it's not what most people think)
Preview text
The four required elements most "TBL" classrooms are missing.

Hi {{ person.first_name }},

Thanks for signing up for the TBL primer. Over the next five emails, I'm going to walk you through what Team-Based Learning actually is, where most implementations fail, and how to build your first module without burning forty hours of your weekends.

Let's start with the most common misconception: TBL is not just active learning with groups.

Active learning is a broad category. Flipping the classroom, problem-based learning, case-based learning, think-pair-share. They all qualify, and they all have evidence supporting them. But Team-Based Learning is something more specific. It has a name, a structure, and a research base going back to the 1980s when Larry Michaelsen developed it at the University of Oklahoma.

A TBL session has four required elements:

  1. Pre-class preparation. Students study assigned material before class. This isn't optional. The classroom time is not for content delivery.
  2. Readiness Assurance Tests. When students arrive, they take a short test (the iRAT, Individual Readiness Assurance Test) alone. Then they retake the same test as a team (the tRAT, Team Readiness Assurance Test) and have to reach consensus on each answer. Disagreements between students force productive arguments.
  3. Appeals process. Teams can challenge any iRAT question they think is unfair or ambiguous, in writing, with evidence. This is rigorous, not casual.
  4. Application exercises. The rest of class is spent on significant problems that require application of the prep material to authentic, ambiguous situations. Teams work simultaneously, then defend their reasoning to the class.

Notice what's not on that list: lecture. In a properly run TBL classroom, the instructor doesn't lecture during class time. The instructor designs, facilitates, and clarifies. The cognitive work is on the students.

This is why TBL is harder to implement than "flipped classroom" or "interactive lecture." It demands more of students upfront and reshapes what the instructor does during class. It's also why the research evidence on TBL is stronger than most active learning interventions: when faculty actually implement it correctly, students retain more, perform better on board exams, and develop the team-based skills clinical practice requires.

Day 2 · 75-minute session breakdown
Subject line
Inside a 75-minute TBL session: the actual time breakdown
Preview text
Minute by minute: what students do and what you do.

Hi {{ person.first_name }},

Yesterday I explained what TBL actually is. Today let's get concrete: what does a real TBL class period look like, minute by minute?

Here's a typical 75-minute session for a medical or health science course:

Minutes 0 to 3. Settle and review. Students arrive, find their teams (TBL teams are stable: same 5 to 7 students all semester). The instructor opens with the day's learning objectives and the day's question: what are we trying to apply today?

Minutes 3 to 15. iRAT (Individual Readiness Assurance Test). Each student takes a 10 to 15 question multiple-choice test individually. Closed-book. The questions cover the assigned pre-class material, but more importantly they target conceptual understanding, not memorization. A good iRAT question can't be answered by skimming the chapter the night before.

Minutes 15 to 30. tRAT (Team Readiness Assurance Test). Same test, same questions, but teams now answer together. Teams use scratch-off cards or digital tools that give immediate feedback. This is where the productive arguments happen. "Why did you pick C?" "Because…" When teams hear each other's reasoning, the wrong answers get corrected before they harden.

Minutes 30 to 35. Appeals. Teams that want to challenge a question submit a written appeal with evidence (a page reference, a citation, a logical argument). Teams that succeed get points back. This builds a culture of evidence-based reasoning.

Minutes 35 to 45. Mini-clarification. The instructor addresses common errors revealed by the RAT scores. This is targeted, not a re-lecture. 10 minutes maximum.

Minutes 45 to 70. Application exercise. Now the real work begins. Teams work on a significant problem that requires applying what they prepped on. The exercise uses what TBL calls the four S's: same problem (every team works on it), specific choice (teams pick a single answer), simultaneous report (every team reveals their answer at the same time), and significant problem (it actually matters).

Minutes 70 to 75. Cross-team discussion. When all teams reveal their choices, the differences spark argument. The instructor facilitates without taking sides until consensus or productive disagreement is reached.

That's it. No lecture. No PowerPoint walking through definitions. The students arrive prepared (or they don't, and the iRAT shows it), then spend class time wrestling with application.

The hard part isn't running a session like this. The hard part is designing the pre-class materials, the RAT questions, and the application exercise so they all line up. That's where most TBL implementations fall apart, and that's what I'll cover tomorrow.

Day 4 · Three failure modes
Subject line
Why most "TBL" classrooms aren't actually TBL
Preview text
Three common failure modes you can spot in your own classroom.

Hi {{ person.first_name }},

Faculty tell me they're running TBL in their classroom. Then I ask three questions and find out they're running something else.

Here are the three most common failure modes I see, and how to spot them in your own classroom.

Failure mode 1: Memory-recall RAT questions.

A good iRAT question asks: "A 45-year-old patient presents with X, Y, and Z. The mechanism behind their presentation is most likely:" with four plausible options that each require understanding the prep material to distinguish.

A bad iRAT question asks: "Which enzyme catalyzes the conversion of pyruvate to lactate?"

The bad question rewards last-minute cramming. The good question requires the conceptual understanding that comes from genuine prep. If your iRAT is mostly bad questions, your students will figure out they can skip the prep and just cram the night before. Pre-class engagement collapses, and TBL stops being TBL.

Failure mode 2: Application exercises that aren't actually applied.

A real application exercise places students in a situation that has no clean right answer, and forces them to take a specific position and defend it.

A fake application exercise asks students to review four cases and identify which one shows hypertension. (That's pattern recognition, not application.)

Better: "Here are four 65-year-old male patients with elevated blood pressure. Resources are limited. Which one would you treat first, and what reasoning drives that choice?" Now teams have to weigh, prioritize, defend.

Failure mode 3: Teams that aren't really teams.

TBL teams are stable. Same 5 to 7 students, all semester, picked by the instructor (not self-selected, not random week-to-week). Stable teams develop trust, productive conflict, and shared accountability over time. They get better at disagreeing well.

Self-selected groups become friend groups. Random rotation prevents trust from forming. Either kills the cohort dynamics that make TBL work.

I want to be honest about something. Most "TBL" implementations I see have at least one of these failure modes, and many have all three. It's not because faculty are doing it wrong on purpose. It's because designing good RAT questions, good application exercises, and good team structures is hard, and there are very few resources that teach you how to do it well.

Email 04 · Day 6
Internal title
Day 6 · Where to start building
Subject line
Building your first TBL module: where to start
Preview text
Start at the end. Five steps, in order.

Hi {{ person.first_name }},

You've seen what TBL actually is and where most implementations fall apart. Today: where to start when you're building your first module.

You don't start with the slides. You don't start with the prep reading. You start at the end and work backward.

Step 1: Pick the application exercise first.

The application exercise is the cognitive payoff of the session. Everything else exists to make it possible. So start there.

What real-world decision do you want students to be able to make at the end of this session? Be specific. Not "understand glomerular filtration" but "given a patient's labs, decide whether they need a nephrology consult." Not "know the muscles of the brachial plexus" but "given an injury pattern, predict which nerve is involved."

Write that decision down. That's your application exercise.

Step 2: Reverse-engineer the prep work.

Now ask: what does a student need to know coming in, in order to participate in that application exercise? Not "what's in the textbook chapter." What's the minimum essential knowledge that lets them weigh options and defend a position?

That's your assigned pre-class reading.

Step 3: Write the RAT questions.

For each major concept in the prep work, write one or two questions that test conceptual understanding, not recall. Use the iRAT to detect whether students actually engaged with the prep, and to flag the misconceptions you'll address in your mini-clarification.

A good rule: if a student could correctly answer your RAT question by Googling, it's not a good RAT question.

Step 4: Form teams and write team-application instructions.

Decide team size (5 to 7 is the sweet spot for medical education). Decide team composition (stratify for diversity of background and experience, don't put all the strongest students together). Write a clear team-application worksheet that frames the exercise, lists the constraints, and requires a specific choice with reasoning.

Step 5: Run a dry test.

Walk through your own session in your head. Time it. Does it fit in your class period? Is there enough time for the application exercise? Are the RAT questions actually requiring the prep work, or could a savvy student fake them?

This is the part where the design usually breaks. Times don't add up. The application exercise turns out to be too narrow or too broad. The RAT questions are too easy.

This is also where I lose most faculty. They spend 30 hours on Steps 1 to 4, hit the dry test, realize the design has holes, and either abandon TBL or ship a flawed first module.

Setting up the sequence in Kajabi.

1. Create the opt-in form

In Kajabi: Sales → Forms → New Form. Name it "TBL Primer Opt-In." Collect first name + email only (any more fields drops conversion). Set the post-submit redirect to a "thank you" Kajabi landing page that confirms Email 01 is on its way.

2. Build the Email Sequence

In Kajabi: Marketing → Email Sequences → New Sequence. Name it "TBL Primer." Add 5 emails with delay triggers:

  • Email 01: Send 0 days after form submission
  • Email 02: Send 1 day after Email 01
  • Email 03: Send 2 days after Email 02 (Day 4 total)
  • Email 04: Send 2 days after Email 03 (Day 6 total)
  • Email 05: Send 2 days after Email 04 (Day 8 total)

Link the Email Sequence to the form so a new submission triggers Email 01 automatically.

3. Personalization syntax

Every email uses {{ person.first_name }}, which is Kajabi's native personalization variable. In Kajabi's email builder, set a fallback of "there" so subscribers without a first name get "Hi there" instead of "Hi {{ person.first_name }}." Test this with a personal email address that doesn't have the field populated.

4. Sender setup

In Kajabi: Settings → Email Settings. Verify the sender domain medmasterscollaborative.com is authenticated (SPF, DKIM, DMARC). Set the from-name as "Sharilyn Rennie" and the reply-to as srennie@medmasterscollaborative.com. The last email explicitly invites replies, and email replies are a strong sender-reputation signal that helps future emails land in inboxes rather than promotions tabs.

5. Where the opt-in form lives

Two options for connecting the primer to your GitHub-hosted homepage:

  • Option A (simplest): Create a Kajabi Landing Page at medmasterscollaborative.com/free-primer, embed the form there, and update the homepage CTAs to link to that URL.
  • Option B: Get the Kajabi form embed code (an HTML snippet) and drop it directly into the homepage. More integrated, but requires the form to play nicely with your homepage CSS.

Option A is faster to launch. Option B is cleaner long term.

6. Tagging for post-sequence branching

Kajabi tags are simpler than Kit or ConvertKit, but they work. After Email 05 sends, you want to identify two groups: those who clicked through to programs.html (warm leads) and those who didn't (cold). In Kajabi's email editor, add a tag like "clicked-programs-page" to any link that goes to programs.html. After the sequence completes, you can segment future broadcasts based on that tag.

7. Subject line A/B testing

Kajabi doesn't have native A/B testing for Email Sequences, but you can manually test by duplicating the sequence with a variant subject line and splitting your traffic. The Email 03 subject ("Why most 'TBL' classrooms aren't actually TBL") is the strongest pattern interrupt and the best candidate if you want to test one. The other subject lines are designed to feel like specific lessons rather than promotional emails.

8. After the primer ends

Subscribers who don't book a Project Lab should move to a "broader nurture" list (monthly newsletter, new content, cohort announcements). Subscribers who DO book should go into a "founding cohort onboarding" sequence with pre-session prep materials. In Kajabi, this is handled by tagging on link clicks during the final email and setting up follow-up sequences triggered by those tags.

Optional Email 06

About six weeks after cohort 1 runs, a sixth email titled "What I learned shipping cohort 1" is a strong follow-up. It can include real testimonials from founding members, what changed in your design, and a renewed CTA for the next cohort. That email writes itself once cohort 1 is done.
