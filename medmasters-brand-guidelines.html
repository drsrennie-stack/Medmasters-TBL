Color palette

Five colors. Two surfaces. One rule.

The palette is small and disciplined. Each color has one job, and the same color does different work depending on whether the surface is light or dark.

White
#FFFFFF
Primary surface. Used for hero, content sections, light pages. The default canvas.
Dark navy
#060A18
Dark surface backgrounds: footer, contrast sections, problem framing, final CTA. Near-black with a navy temperature.
Navy
#0B1530
Primary text on light surfaces. Headlines, body, eyebrows. Slightly lifted from the bg navy so type sits forward.
Terra cotta
#8B3A2E
The brand accent. Headline accent words, primary CTA button fill, "Masters" wordmark. Carries the warmth.
Gold
#C9A14A
Small structural accent on dark surfaces only. Eyebrows, footer column headers, micro-labels. Replaces terra cotta where contrast demands.
Cream
#F5F1E8
Body text on dark surfaces. Never used as a background, only for type, fills, and figures on dark navy.
The split rule
Terra cotta owns the large rhetorical accents and primary buttons on every surface. Gold takes over for small structural text (eyebrows, micro-labels) on dark surfaces only, where terra cotta fails contrast minimums against near-black.

Writing rules

How the brand sounds.

The voice is warm, academic, and direct. These rules keep it consistent across every page, email, slide, and post.

01
Never use em dashes.
The em dash is banned from every brand surface. Use a period for hard stops, a colon for setup, or restructure the sentence. This rule is non-negotiable. Use a period. Or a colon: like this. Use an em dash — like this.
02
Never italicize Collaborative.
The word "Collaborative" is part of the brand wordmark. It always sits in spaced caps as an eyebrow beneath "MedMasters." Italics flatten its visual role. COLLABORATIVE (spaced caps, no italic) Collaborative (never italic)
03
Eyebrows are spaced caps. No dash.
Every section opens with an eyebrow in 11px terra cotta (light surface) or gold (dark surface), letter-spaced 0.30em, uppercase. No decorative dash, arrow, or bullet before the eyebrow text. FACULTY QUESTIONS — Faculty Questions
04
Color the key words in headlines.
Every headline has one or two terra cotta accent words. The accent word carries the rhetorical weight: the verb you want them to feel, the noun you want them to remember. Build a deployable TBL component in 90 minutes. Build a deployable TBL component in 90 minutes.
05
Short sentences. Short paragraphs.
Faculty buyers are time-poor. Every section earns its place. Lead with the claim. Prove it in one or two sentences. Stop. The rhythm matters more than completeness.
06
Numbers do real work.
Use specific numbers wherever you can. "90 minutes." "30 days." "8 seats." "$87." "10 monthly build cycles." Specificity creates trust. Vague claims feel like marketing.
07
No bookend pastel callouts.
Cards that need to stand apart use white surfaces, hairline borders, and soft drop shadows that lift on hover. The bookend pattern (pastel cream or pastel gold background with a vertical colored bar) is reserved for internal planning documents only, never customer-facing pages. White card. Hairline border. Soft shadow. Lifts on hover. Cream background with terra cotta or gold bar on the left.
08
Tier scope is sacred.
Project Lab produces one component. Sprint produces one complete integrated module. Annual produces a system across multiple modules. Never let marketing copy claim Lab builds a module, or Sprint builds a system. Each tier owns its own scope, and the pricing ladder depends on the distinction. Build a deployable TBL component in 90 minutes. (Lab) Build a deployable TBL module in 90 minutes. (Lab claiming Sprint's scope)

Brand-locked exceptions

Three places where the rule breaks on purpose.

The split rule (terra cotta on light, gold on dark for small structural text) has three permanent exceptions. These exist because brand identity outranks contrast harmony in specific places.

LOCKED
Exception 01
"Masters" always terra cotta.
Inside the wordmark "MedMasters," the word "Masters" is always terra cotta, on every surface. This is brand identity, not styling. Even on dark navy where gold would have better contrast, "Masters" stays terra cotta to keep the wordmark immediately recognizable.
LOCKED
Exception 02
Primary buttons always terra cotta.
The filled primary CTA button is terra cotta on every surface. White or cream text sits on top. This is the conversion pattern, the eye magnet, the trail of breadcrumbs through the site. Gold buttons would dilute the consistency.
LOCKED
Exception 03
Big headline accents stay terra cotta on dark.
When a headline accent word appears at 30+px on a dark surface, it stays terra cotta. Contrast holds at that scale, and the rhetorical weight of the brand color does its job. The gold-replacement rule only kicks in at small body-text scales (under ~16px).
